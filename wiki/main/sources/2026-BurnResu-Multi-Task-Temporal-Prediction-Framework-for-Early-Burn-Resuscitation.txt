Abstract:
Burn shock is a leading cause of early mortality in patients with severe burns, and fluid
resuscitation requires balancing the competing risks of hypoperfusion and fluid
overload. Although empirical formulas are widely used for initial guidance, they provide
population-level estimates and fail to adapt to the evolving hemodynamic status of
individual patients. In this study, we developed and internally validated BurnResu, a
hierarchical temporal deep learning framework that jointly predicts fluid type and
infusion rate for the first 72 hours after injury. Using retrospective data from 364
patients with severe burns, the model integrates baseline characteristics, hourly vital
signs, and fluid-administration histories in a rolling prediction paradigm: at each time
point, all information available up to that point is used to predict clinician resuscitation
decisions in the subsequent hour. BurnResu employs multi-scale temporal encoding to
capture both rapid hemodynamic responses and gradual volume shifts, conditions
these representations on patient-specific baseline features, and couples classification
and regression heads to jointly determine fluid selection and dosing. The training
objective enforces cumulative dose fidelity and penalizes physiologically implausible
infusion trajectories, reflecting the clinical priority of overall volume adequacy over
pointwise accuracy. On an independent test cohort, BurnResu achieved an AUC of
0.816 for fluid-type prediction, with dose-estimation performance of MAE = 27.44 mL/h,
R² = 0.820, and RMSE = 60.04 mL/h.

Early fluid resuscitation after severe burns is a representative real-world expert decision-making
problem. During the first 72 hours after injury, clinicians must continuously determine both the type and
rate of fluid infusion by integrating burn severity, physiological responses, urine output, and prior fluid-
administration history. Although empirical formulas such as Parkland and Brooke provide useful initial
guidance, they are population-level estimates and do not fully adapt to patient-specific hemodynamic
evolution. This makes early burn resuscitation a meaningful scenario for developing intelligent expert
systems that combine data-driven temporal learning with domain knowledge.
From the perspective of expert systems and applied artificial intelligence, this task presents several
challenges. The data are heterogeneous and sequential, including baseline characteristics, hourly vital
signs, urine output, and historical fluid administration. The decision target is structured: the system
needs to infer both categorical fluid-type selection and continuous infusion-rate estimation. In addition,
clinically acceptable predictions require temporal coherence and physiological plausibility, because
abrupt or inconsistent infusion recommendations may be difficult to interpret or apply in practice.
To address these challenges, we propose BurnResu, a hierarchical temporal deep learning framework
for early burn resuscitation. BurnResu learns from real-world clinician treatment records while incorpo-
rating clinically guided temporal regularization to improve decision plausibility. The model integrates
patient baseline characteristics, hourly physiological observations, and previous fluid-administration his-
tories in a rolling prediction paradigm. At each time point, only information available up to that time is
used to predict clinician resuscitation decisions in the subsequent hour. The framework jointly predicts

Methodologically, BurnResu captures multi-scale temporal dependencies through patient-specific
baseline encoding, multi-scale temporal modeling, and conditioned decoding. The temporal encoder
captures both rapid hemodynamic responses and gradual volume shifts, while patient-specific condition-

inhalation injury. The decision-first training objective couples instantaneous prediction accuracy with
cumulative dose fidelity and temporal regularization, encouraging smoother and more physiologically
plausible infusion trajectories.
We evaluated BurnResu using retrospective data from 364 patients with severe burns. Data splitting
was performed at the patient level, and all preprocessing procedures were fitted exclusively on the training
set to avoid information leakage. On an independent test cohort, BurnResu achieved a macro-AUC of
0.816 for fluid-type prediction, with an MAE of 27.44 mL/h, an R2 of 0.820, and an RMSE of 60.04 mL/h
for infusion-rate estimation. The proposed framework showed advantages over traditional formula-based
references, representative machine-learning models, and deep-learning baselines.
We also emphasize that this study should be interpreted as clinician-decision modeling rather than
a treatment-effectiveness study. The prediction targets were derived from real-world clinician actions
rather than randomized treatment comparisons.
Therefore, the model reflects current resuscitation

prove superiority over expert care. This distinction is explicitly discussed in the manuscript.

1. Introduction
Burn injuries represent a major global health burden, occurring in civilian, industrial, and mass casualty settings
and causing substantial physical, psychological, and economic burden(James et al., 2020). In patients with severe burns
(total body surface area, TBSA > 30%), extensive capillary leakage leads to marked intravascular fluid loss and burn
shock—a leading cause of early mortality(Cartotto et al., 2022). Effective fluid resuscitation within the first 48 hours
after injury is critical to prevent hypoperfusion and organ failure; however, inadequate or excessive fluid administration
can deepen burn wounds, prolong the shock phase, and increase mortality(Guilabert et al., 2016; Greenhalgh, 2019).
In clinical practice, resuscitation is typically guided by empirical formulas (e.g., Parkland, Brooke) that estimate
fluid requirements from body weight and burn size(Baxter and Shires, 1968; Diver, 2008). While simple and widely
applicable, these formulas do not account for inter-patient variability or evolving hemodynamics, and their accuracy
depends on TBSA estimation, which is often error-prone in non-specialist settings(Kahn et al., 2010; Parvizi et al.,
2014). In resource-limited settings where burn specialists are unavailable, delayed or suboptimal resuscitation can lead
to shock progression and poor clinical outcomes(Zhu et al., 2020). These limitations motivate data-driven decision-
support systems that integrate dynamic physiological information for individualized fluid management(Alvarado et al.,
2009; Burn and Trauma Branch of Chinese Geriatrics Society, 2020).

Prior computational efforts, including rule-based decision-support systems and urine-output–driven titration
models, have shown promise for standardizing resuscitation; however, these approaches do not leverage modern deep
learning to jointly model the full complexity of multimodal temporal data, nor do they capture patient-specific temporal
dynamics at scale.
Recent advances in temporal deep learning—including temporal convolutional networks, Transformers, and multi-
task architectures—have shown considerable potential for modeling complex, non-stationary physiological time
series in critical care(Benes et al., 2014; Chiao et al., 2018). By learning from multimodal data (vital signs, fluid
administration histories, baseline characteristics), deep learning models can capture subtle inter-individual variability
that formula-based methods miss. In burn resuscitation, fluid requirements evolve over time in response to multiple
interacting physiological factors, making the problem well-suited for such approaches. In this study, we developed
BurnResu, a hierarchical temporal deep learning framework that jointly predicts fluid types and infusion rates for
individualized early burn resuscitation by modeling physiological dynamics at multiple time scales and conditioning
predictions on patient-specific baseline characteristics. The main contributions of this study are as follows:
• Construction of a real-world, multimodal dataset specifically designed for early burn resuscitation;
• Development of BurnResu, a hierarchical temporal prediction framework for fluid-type classification and dose
prediction;
• Evaluation showing that the model achieves favorable performance relative to traditional resuscitation formulas
and representative machine-learning and deep-learning methods.
2. Related Works
2.1. Computational Fluid Resuscitation
Computational approaches to burn fluid resuscitation remain limited. The open-loop CDSS of Salinas et al.(Salinas
et al., 2011) models crystalloid infusion with exponential decay curves and urine-output–guided adjustments but lacks
patient-specific temporal modeling of dynamic physiological trajectories. Luo et al.(Luo et al., 2015) proposed a
urine-output–driven titration model, demonstrating that adaptive, physiology-responsive strategies can improve fluid
management. Prior to these efforts, several empirical resuscitation formulas have been developed and widely adopted,
including the Parkland, Brooke, Evans, and TMMU formulas(Baxter and Shires, 1968; Diver, 2008; Kahn et al., 2010),
which estimate fluid requirements from body weight and burn size but do not incorporate real-time physiological
feedback. Table 1 summarizes the most commonly used burn resuscitation formulas and their fluid-type–specific dosing
strategies across the first 48 hours post-injury.

Despite these advances, existing computational methods are primarily rule-based or regression-driven and do not
leverage deep learning to jointly model multimodal temporal data. No prior approach has addressed the simultaneous

2.2. Multi-Task Learning for Joint Classification and Regression in Healthcare
Clinical prediction tasks frequently involve coupled decisions—for instance, determining whether to administer
a treatment (classification) and at what dose (regression). Multi-task learning(Caruana, 1997) provides a principled
framework for jointly optimizing such heterogeneous objectives by sharing representations across related tasks, which
can improve generalization compared with training independent single-task models. In healthcare, joint classification–
regression architectures have been applied to problems such as predicting mortality risk alongside length of stay,
detecting arrhythmia type while estimating its severity, and identifying the need for mechanical ventilation together
with expected duration. These formulations reflect the clinical reality that treatment decisions are interdependent: the
choice of intervention and its intensity are governed by overlapping physiological determinants and should be modeled
accordingly. In burn resuscitation, fluid-type selection (crystalloid, colloid, or glucose) and infusion-rate estimation are
inherently coupled, as the clinical rationale for choosing a particular fluid directly informs the expected dosing range.
Our work extends the joint classification–regression paradigm to this domain, where shared temporal representations
capture the physiological context driving both decisions.

3. Methods
3.1. Study Design and Population
This retrospective study used de-identified clinical data from the Institute of Burn Research, Southwest Hospital,
Third Military Medical University (Army Medical University), Chongqing, China, for patients admitted between July
1, 2016 and December 31, 2023. Eligible patients had total burn surface area (TBSA) greater than 30%; burn injuries
caused by flames, explosions, hydrothermal fluids, or electricity; and admission within 48 hours after injury. Patients
with incomplete medical records or those not admitted to the ICU were excluded. After screening, 364 cases met the
inclusion criteria. The study protocol was approved by the Ethics Committee of Southwest Hospital (KY2025135),
and all procedures complied with the Declaration of Helsinki and relevant national regulations.
3.2. Dataset Construction
To construct a dataset tailored for burn shock resuscitation, we implemented a systematic data-processing pipeline
consisting of data acquisition, annotation and processing, and final dataset assembly. During data acquisition, multi-
source medical data were collected and integrated. These included baseline patient information (e.g., TBSA, clinical
interventions, treatment protocols), electronic medical records (EMRs), and high-frequency vital-sign monitoring data
such as heart rate, blood pressure, and oxygen saturation. For temporal modeling, each patient contributed multiple
hourly prediction windows within the first 72 hours post-injury, yielding a substantially larger number of time-indexed
training samples. Data splitting was performed at the patient level to ensure that all prediction windows from a given
patient were assigned exclusively to the training, validation, or test set.
3.3. Dataset Annotation and Integration
As shown in Fig. 1, annotation and structuring were carried out using a human–machine collaborative approach.
Target information was extracted from the integrated EMRs and subsequently processed through data cleaning, error
filtering, and imputation. A team of experienced burn specialists standardized key clinical parameters, including
infusion rates and resuscitation fluid volumes. A fine-tuned ChatGLM3-6B model was deployed to assist automated
named-entity recognition, extracting medication names, dosages, and administration times from unstructured clinical
notes. All LLM-extracted entities were verified by two independent clinicians before inclusion. To ensure data quality,
a double-blind annotation verification process was performed. Baseline vital-sign time-series were then temporally
aligned, and outliers were corrected.
In the final dataset generation stage, multimodal data-fusion techniques were used to integrate structured clinical
data with high-frequency vital-sign monitoring data along a unified timeline. This process produced a cohesive
spatiotemporal dataset suitable for downstream modeling. The resulting dataset supports efficient timestamp-based

3.4. Fluid Resuscitation Prediction Model Development
All predictor variables were limited to information available up to the prediction time point. Baseline features
were recorded at admission, while time-varying physiological measurements and fluid administration histories were
temporally aligned and truncated to prevent information leakage. Outcomes were defined exclusively based on clinician
actions occurring after the prediction time point, ensuring strict temporal separation between predictors and outcomes.
Missing values in real-world time-series data were handled using a separate neural network–based imputation model
trained solely on the training set and applied in a forward-only manner using past and contemporaneous observations.
To model the dynamic and patient-specific nature of burn shock resuscitation, we developed a hierarchical temporal
architecture that mirrors the clinical reasoning process: establishing a patient-specific physiological baseline, tracking
hemodynamic evolution across multiple time scales, and modulating fluid decisions based on both immediate dynamics
and the patient’s overall trajectory. The framework (Fig. 2) receives multivariate time-series inputs 𝐗∈ℝ𝐻×𝐷𝑇and
static baseline features 𝐛∈ℝ𝐷𝐵, and comprises three stages—a Baseline Encoder, a Multi-Scale Temporal Encoder,
and a Conditioned Decoder—that jointly predict fluid type and infusion rate.

3.5. Loss Function

outcomes. We address this with a decision-first training objective that evaluates predictions at the trajectory level,
coupling instantaneous accuracy with cumulative dose fidelity—reflecting how clinicians titrate fluids by tracking
overall volume adequacy rather than optimizing each hourly adjustment in isolation.

3.6. Model Training and Evaluation
The model was implemented in PyTorch 1.13 and trained on an NVIDIA A100 GPU using the Adam optimizer
with an initial learning rate of 1 × 10−3 and a StepLR decay schedule every 100 epochs. Models were trained for up to
500 epochs with early stopping based on validation performance. Patient-level data were randomly split into training,
validation, and test sets (70%/15%/15%), and all preprocessing—including robust per-class scaling and a separate
neural imputation model—was fitted exclusively on the training set to prevent leakage.
During validation, temperature scaling and decision-threshold optimization were applied, and the resulting
thresholds were fixed for testing. Cumulative targets were computed causally, and instantaneous predictions were
clipped at a clinically defined maximum infusion rate. Robustness was assessed using alternative Focal loss(Lin et al.,
2017) and cosine warm-restart schedules.
Model evaluation used Macro-F1 as the primary classification metric and area under the receiver operating
characteristic curve (AUC), precision, and recall as secondary metrics. Regression performance was assessed via mean
absolute error (MAE), coefficient of determination (𝑅2), and root mean squared error (RMSE), computed over positive-
flow periods. The dataset comprised longitudinal treatment sequences from 364 patients. On an NVIDIA A100 GPU,
average inference time was under 5 seconds per patient for a full 72-hour trajectory.
3.7. Statistical Analysis
Continuous variables were summarized as median values with interquartile ranges (IQRs), and categorical variables
were presented as frequencies and percentages. Group differences were assessed using the Mann–Whitney U test for
continuous variables and the chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test for categorical variables, as appropriate. Associations
between baseline characteristics and total infused fluid volume were assessed via Pearson and Spearman correlation,
with results reported as standardized effect sizes in Table 2. Categorical variables describing fluid types (e.g.,
crystalloid, colloid, glucose) were summarized descriptively by administration frequency.

5. Discussion
This study demonstrates that temporal deep learning can predict fluid resuscitation requirements from real-world
burn-care data despite irregular sampling, missing values, and substantial patient heterogeneity. The proposed model
achieved competitive classification and regression performance, indicating that multi-scale temporal architectures can
capture the evolving physiological dynamics that drive fluid needs.
Treating resuscitation as a continuous sequence rather than as independent time points was critical to model perfor-
mance. The ablation results show that removing temporal encoding caused the largest drop across all metrics (Table 4),

confirming that sequential physiological dynamics—not static baseline features alone—drive fluid requirements. This
aligns with the clinical trajectory of burn shock, where hemodynamic status evolves over hours and resuscitation must
track these changes.
From an architectural perspective, three design choices proved important. The dual-path dilated TCN captured
multi-resolution temporal features that pure LSTM or Transformer baselines could not match on this moderate-sized
clinical cohort(Bai et al., 2018; Hochreiter and Schmidhuber, 1997). FiLM-based conditioning(Perez et al., 2018)
enabled patient-specific adaptation without substantially increasing parameters, yielding consistent gains over simple
feature concatenation. The joint classification–regression formulation leveraged the inherent coupling between fluid-
type selection and dose estimation, consistent with findings that multi-task learning improves generalization when
tasks are semantically related(Caruana, 1997).
Nevertheless, the present study should be interpreted as a clinician-decision modeling study rather than a treatment-
effectiveness study. The model learns from retrospective clinician actions recorded in real-world care and therefore
reflects practice patterns rather than proven optimal therapy. Consequently, although the model shows favorable
predictive performance, these results do not establish superiority over expert care or prove causal benefit for patient
outcomes. Prospective studies remain necessary before any clinical deployment.
Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the current model is purely supervised and depends heavily
on the availability and quality of labeled data. In clinical contexts, generating high-quality annotations can be time-
consuming and costly. Future work may therefore explore self-supervised or reinforcement-learning-based approaches
to mitigate data scarcity and better align model behavior with long-term patient outcomes. Second, this study relied
on retrospective single-center data, which limits generalizability. Incorporating multi-center datasets and introducing
multi-resolution temporal representations, uncertainty estimation, and domain knowledge (e.g., clinical guidelines
and expert heuristics) could enhance interpretability, robustness, and reliability. Extending the dataset to include
multimodal sources such as imaging, laboratory, and physiological signals will also be important for validating the
model across diverse patient populations.
6. Conclusions
BurnResu provides a data-driven framework for early burn resuscitation that jointly predicts fluid type and infusion
rate from multimodal temporal data. On a retrospective cohort of 364 patients, the model captured dynamic patient-
state evolution and delivered accurate predictions for both classification and regression endpoints. These results
demonstrate that temporally aware deep learning can manage the complexity and irregularity of real-world clinical
time series, supporting the integration of such models into critical-care decision-support systems. The source code
