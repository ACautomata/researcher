Abstract. Training Scene Graph Generation (SGG) models with natu-
ral language captions has become increasingly popular due to the abun-
dant, cost-effective, and open-world generalization supervision signals
that natural language offers. However, such unstructured caption data
and its processing pose significant challenges in learning accurate and
comprehensive scene graphs. The challenges can be summarized as three
aspects: 1) traditional scene graph parsers based on linguistic represen-
tation often fail to extract meaningful relationship triplets from caption
data. 2) grounding unlocalized objects of parsed triplets will meet ambi-
guity issues in visual-language alignment. 3) caption data typically are
sparse and exhibit bias to partial observations of image content. Aim-
ing to address these problems, we propose a divide-and-conquer strategy
with a novel framework named GPT4SGG, to obtain more accurate and
comprehensive scene graph signals. This framework decomposes a com-
plex scene into a bunch of simple regions, resulting in a set of region-
specific narratives. With these region-specific narratives (partial observa-
tions) and a holistic narrative (global observation) for an image, a large
language model (LLM) performs the relationship reasoning to synthe-
size an accurate and comprehensive scene graph. Experimental results
demonstrate GPT4SGG significantly improves the performance of SGG
models trained on image-caption data, in which the ambiguity issue and
long-tail bias have been well-handled with more accurate and compre-
hensive scene graphs.

In contrast to conventional methods [5,18,41,42] , our framework, GPT4SGG,
innovates by reversing the pipeline of language-supervised SGG, as shown in
Fig. 2. Previous approaches typically follow the pipeline: (1) parsing relationship
triplets from caption data →(2) grounding unlocalized objects of parsed triplets
→(3) learning scene graphs with parsed triplets and grounded objects. However,
this pipeline faces significant challenges discussed before. For instance, given
a parsed triplet <person, on, tie>, it is very difficult to choose which object
is our target only based on the word “person” and the visual input, although
the grounding model knows there are a man and a woman in this image. Our
framework addresses these issues by:
1. (Step 1: Grounding) Prioritizing the localization of objects using annotations
or object detectors, ensuring accurate visual-language alignment ;
2. (Step 2: Decomposing) Decomposing a complex scene into a set of regions.
Specifically, both holistic and region-specific narratives are generated to in-
form the relationship reasoning process, thereby mitigating caption data
biases ;
3. (Step 3: Relation Reasoning) Utilizing localized objects and a set of obser-
vations (narratives) as input for LLMs like GPT-4, allowing for more precise
relationship deduction between objects.
By utilizing this divide-and-conquer strategy, GPT4SGG substantially enhances
the accuracy and comprehensiveness of the generated scene graphs, as our ex-
periments demonstrate.

Related Work
Scene Graph Generation (SGG) aims to create a structured representa-
tion of objects and their relationships within an image. Johnson et al. [11] first
introduced the concept of scene graphs, presenting them as a method to im-
prove image retrieval and scene understanding. The evolution of SGG has been
marked by an increasing focus on extracting more detailed and contextually rich
information from visual content. This field has expanded significantly with con-
tributions from both vision-based and language-based methodologies, providing
a comprehensive understanding of scene composition and dynamics. Earlier ap-
proaches to SGG predominantly leveraged deep learning models to identify and
classify objects and their interrelations within images. These methods, such as
IMP [37], MOTIFS [40], primarily depended on convolutional neural networks
(CNNs) or recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to extract visual features. How-
ever, the reliance on manually annotated data limits the scope of relationship
recognition.
Recent works [5,41,42] integrate natural language processing into SGG, pro-
viding a cheap yet valuable scheme for capturing complex relationships. These
models could learn rich relationship concepts without manual annotations by em-
ploying image-caption pairs. This approach, while effective in certain contexts,
faced limitations in scalability and the diversity of relationship types it could

capture. Previous methods of learning scene graphs from image-caption data are
mainly struggled by the three challenges discussed in Sec. 1. This work reverses
previous pipeline and presents a novel framework to address these challenges.
Large Language Model (LLM) has gained increasing attention on many
complex reasoning tasks, including multi-hop question answering [14], multi-
turn conversation [36], program synthesis [24], etc. Among a series of LLMs,
GPT-4 [26], developed by OpenAI, presents a remarkable capability to solve
complex reasoning tasks. As reported in [1], GPT-4 can solve many novel and
challenging tasks such as mathematics, medicine, finance, law, etc. These ap-
plications showcase the great potentiality of GPT-4 with task-specific prompts.
Beyond textual data, the recently released GPT-4V [27] can access image input
and perform various visual reasoning tasks. Nevertheless, the vision model of
GPT-4V remains like a black box, with its API currently limited in speed and
usage (as of February 2024).
Beyond GPT-4, the open-sourced Llama2 [33] and its variants have emerged
for various applications, especially for vision tasks. For instance, Mini GPT-
4 [43] utilizes Vicuna [6] and a frozen vision encoder to learn a multi-modality
alignment, enabling the generation of detailed image descriptions, website cre-
ation from sketches, and other complex reasoning tasks; LLaVA [20] leverages
visual instruction tuning with language-only GPT-4 and a frozen vision encoder,
enabling advanced multi-modal understanding and interaction capabilities. Re-
gardless of these attempts, the SGG capability of LLMs still needs to be explored.
In this work, we will explore how to generate an accurate and comprehensive
scene graph with the help of LLMs.
Concurrent Work. Parallel to our efforts, the LLM4SGG framework [12] uti-
lizes an LLM to extract triplets from original and paraphrased captions. Despite
its advantage over traditional scene parsers like [22], it still follows the pipeline
discussed in Sec. 1. Thus, it cannot avoid the ambiguity issue in grounding un-
localized objects. Moreover, relying on paraphrasing captions still cannot obtain
comprehensive scene graphs for complex scenes.

3.2
Scene Decomposing: Holistic & Region Narratives Generation
“A picture is worth a thousand words”. An Image of a complex scene has many
plentiful clues and is hard to be summarized with short sentences by captioning
models. An intuitive idea is to “look” an image multiple times, like using sliding
windows in R-CNN [8]. However, with the localized objects, we can reduce the
complexity by selecting representative Region-of-Interest (RoI) areas.
The construction process is shown in Algorithm 1. Specifically, the holistic
narrative is generated by feeding the whole image to the captioning model, pro-
viding a holistic description of the image content ; the region-specific narratives
are produced by captioning a set of RoI areas, focusing on the content of lo-
cal regions, especially the interaction between two objects. To select meaningful
and representative RoIs for an input image, Localized objects are pairwise com-
bined and filtered with an Intersection-over-Union (IoU) greater than zero. This
selection criterion for RoI pairs is grounded in the premise that objects with
a non-zero IoU are more likely to have a meaningful relationship as indicated
in [40,42], which also reduces the computation burden for captioning and com-
plexity for LLM’s inference. By leveraging object categories, spatial locations,
holistic narratives, and region-specific descriptions, LLMs can “see” images and
interpret images without direct visual data input, facilitating complex visual
reasoning tasks like SGG.
Another advantage of this textual representation for images is that caption-
ing models pre-trained on large-scale datasets typically receive a relatively small
input resolution like 224 × 224 in BLIP-2 [15], while this textual representation
is independent of image resolution. For images with high-resolution and com-
plex scenes, we can obtain plentiful and meaningful descriptions focusing on
interactions between two objects.

3.3
Relation Reasoning: Synthesizing Scene Graphs with LLM
Given a set of region-specific narratives and a global narrative , we can follow the
previous pipeline (see Fig. 2) to extract relationship triplets using a scene parser.
However, this method will meet with troubles. Take the example in Fig. 3, a cake
made of books can be parsed as (cake, made of, book) but there is no cake in
the image and there are several books. Another challenge is to keep the semantic
consistency, i.e., an unique instance can be captioned in different ways (e.g.,
“man”, “people”, “person” for person.2 in Fig. 3 ) but the model should correctly
associate the alias.
To perform relation reasoning with fewer misjudgments and semantic consis-
tency, we utilize an LLM to synthesize scene graphs from those narratives and
objects’ information. Specifically, we construct a prompt template shown in Tab.
6, which requires the model to use information about objects (such as their cat-
egories and bounding boxes) and both holistic and region-specific descriptions
to establish relationships between objects. To distinguish multiple instances, we
encode the object information as “[cateogry].[number]: [box]” in the prompt. This
unique representation enables the LLM to correctly match visual regions with
relationship triplet, especially when multiple instances with the same category
appear in an image.
The LLM is also required to maintain logical consistency to circumvent im-
possible or nonsensical relationships. With this carefully designed prompt, GPT-
4 can synthesize scene graphs based on the provided image information. An
example of this process is shown in Tab. 7. The example shows that GPT-4

3.4
Training SGG Models
After obtaining the generated scene graphs by an LLM, any standard SGG
models can be trained with localized objects and relationships. A prerequisite
for SGG models under a closed-set setting is that the localized objects and
generated relationships should be mapped into target categories before training.
3.5
Instruction Tuning Private LLMs
We use Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) [10] to fine-tune a private and local LLM,
Llama 2 [33], with the instruction-following data generated by GPT-4. This
process involves adapting Llama 2 to better align with specific instruction sets,
enhancing its ability to synthesize scene graphs from textual image data.

GPT-4: To infer that “person.2” rather than “person.6” is wearing “tie.1”, the following observations
from the image data can be considered:
Spatial Proximity: The bounding box of “tie.1" ([269, 189, 293, 234]) is entirely within the bounding
box of “person.2" ([224, 60, 480, 483]). This indicates that the tie is located on or very near to per-
son.2. In contrast, the bounding box of “person.6" ([57, 143, 254, 638]) does not overlap with that
of the tie, suggesting that person.6 is not wearing it. Global and Localized Descriptions: The global
description mentions a “man and woman standing next to a cake,” but does not specify who is wear-
ing the tie. However, the union of “tie.1” and “person.2” in the captions suggests a relationship be-
tween them. Since ties are typically worn as part of clothing, the logical inference is that person.2
is wearing the tie. Logical Consistency: In typical scenarios, a tie is worn by one person at a time.
Given that the tie’s location coincides with the location of person.2 and not with person.6, it is log-
ical to conclude that person.2 is the one wearing the tie. In conclusion, the spatial arrangement of
the objects and the given descriptions lead to the inference that “person.2” is wearing “tie.1”.

4.2
Experimental Setup
Model settings. we mainly compare two SGG models with GPT4SGG: 1)
VS3 [41] extends the object detector of the SGG model from closed-set to open-
vocabulary. 2) OvSGTR [5] advances the SGG from closed-set to both object
and relation-aware open-vocabulary setting. For a fair comparison, we follow the
official implementations to conduct experiments.
Metric. We use the protocol SGDET [30,37] (also known as SGGen) to mea-
sure the capability of SGG models. Recall@K (R@K, K=20/50/100) and mean
Recall@K (mR@K, K=20/50/100) are reported. mR@K reflects the capability
of SGG models to address long-tail bias.

Qualitative results. We provide samples generated by GPT4SGG and corre-

average recall at IoU threshold 0.5). Besides, as discussed before, a low recall
rate compared to manual annotation does not mean low-quality scene graphs.
4.5
Discussion
This work introduces a novel pipeline to learning scene graphs from natural lan-
guage descriptions. Despite its advantages, there remain some limitations. First,
we do not consider using multi-modal LLMs like GPT-4V to process images
directly. Second, the performance of such a pipeline is highly affected by the
ability of LLMs. Finally, due to the bias of manual annotation and machine gen-
eration, the current evaluation protocol only using recall cannot comprehensively
evaluate the quality of generated scene graphs.
Future work. Limited by computation resources, constructing large-scale SGG
datasets using the open-sourced Llama and existing large-scale object detection
datasets like Open Images [13], LVIS [9] has yet to be explored. We believe such
large-scale SGG pre-training scheme can boost the generalization ability of SGG
models.
5
Conclusion
In this work, we propose a simple yet effective framework, GPT4SGG, to syn-
thesize scene graphs from textual representation for images. This framework
decomposes a complex scene of an image into a set of simple regions to obtain
their descriptions. With these region-specific narratives, a global narrative, and

synthesizing the final scene graph. Our framework effectively addressed the am-

Experimental results demonstrate GPT4SGG can generate more accurate and
comprehensive scene graphs.
